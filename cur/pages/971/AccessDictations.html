Storycorps


Storycorps is an American project to record the stories of everyday Americans. You can hear them each Friday morning on NPR radio.
A lot of the stories are not difficult to understand. So they are suitable for people learning English. At the same time, they are real stories, moving, interesting and likeable.

Non-verbal communication

Personal space and eye contact

Do you know there are rules about interpersonal distance and staring at people? Do these unwritten rules that you unconsciously follow every day in Taiwan apply in all cultures and in virtual worlds, eg Second Life?

Watch this video from NPR's Bryant Park Project in the US, titled Don't stand so close and don't stare at me!.

Smiling

Do Taiwanese people smile during service encounters? Are there differences between the West and Taiwan in when people smile. I remember the wife of a Taiwanese professor smiling when she found out I had hurt my back. She made me feel even worse than I already felt!

Listen to this podcast from a German business English magazine, Business Spotlight about smiling in the US and in Germany

Then login and fill in the blanks in the smiling exercise.

Communication styles

Disagreeing by raising objections

How do you communicate disagreement? What does the other party understand you to mean, when you raise objections to their plan?

Listen to Greg Bissky a Canadian business consultant operating in mainland China, talk about how Westerners and Chinese business people fail to communicate because of their misunderstanding of what the other party believes objections mean, in Western-Chinese Business: Saying No Without Saying No on Youtube.

See his website: http://www.treasuremountain.com/

Transcribe his presentation by logging in and filling in the blanks in the no exercise.

Answer the True/False question which pops up.

Logging in

Note: You need to get a login from Dr Bean to transcribe these conversations.

    Your name is your Chinese name in Chinese characters. Your ID is your school ID.
  • The first letter of the ID, eg U or N, is a capital (big) letter.
  • Your password is the first half of your first name. It is in English, not the Chinese character. Thus JiaQi's pasword is Jia. The first letter of the password is a capital letter. The spelliing of the U in 育 is a big U, not a small u. See Lin YuTang's Chinese-English dictionary.
  • If I was not able to find the first character in your name, your password may be a question mark. Eg, 賴?岑. Other people whose names my computer does not have Chinese characters for are 黃慧? and ?琮婷, but their passwords should be okay.
